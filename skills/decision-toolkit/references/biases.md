# Cognitive Biases Reference

Used by `/decision-toolkit`. These are the biases most likely to distort high-stakes decisions. For each: what it is, how to detect it, and how to counter it.

---

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

**What it is**: Continuing to invest in something because of past investment, not because of future potential.

**Detection questions**:
- "We've already spent [X] on this" — is this influencing the decision?
- Would you make this same choice if you were starting fresh today?
- If someone else showed you this situation, not knowing the history, what would you advise?

**Counter**: Focus only on future costs and benefits. Past investment is gone regardless of what you decide next.

---

## Status Quo Bias

**What it is**: Preferring the current state simply because it's familiar, even when change would be better.

**Detection questions**:
- Is the main argument for the current path "we're used to it" or "it's working well enough"?
- If you were designing this from scratch, would you choose the current approach?
- Are you defending the status quo, or genuinely evaluating it?

**Counter**: Force yourself to evaluate the cost of NOT changing, not just the cost of changing.

---

## Confirmation Bias

**What it is**: Seeking and interpreting information in ways that confirm pre-existing beliefs.

**Detection questions**:
- Have you actively sought out evidence AGAINST your preferred option?
- Did you talk only to people who already agree with you?
- Did you discount data that contradicted your hypothesis?

**Counter**: Explicitly assign someone to argue for the option you're less drawn to. Search for disconfirming evidence first.

---

## Optimism Bias

**What it is**: Overestimating the probability of positive outcomes and underestimating the probability of negative ones.

**Detection questions**:
- Is your best-case scenario what you *want* to happen, or what the data suggests will happen?
- Have similar initiatives in the past taken longer / cost more than planned?
- What does the base rate for this type of decision look like?

**Counter**: Use base rates. If 70% of new product launches miss their revenue targets, don't plan as if you're in the 30%.

---

## Planning Fallacy

**What it is**: Underestimating the time, cost, and risks of a plan while overestimating its benefits.

**Detection questions**:
- Is the timeline based on a best-case scenario?
- Does the budget include a contingency?
- How long and how much did similar past projects take?

**Counter**: Use "reference class forecasting" — look at how long similar projects actually took, then adjust your estimate up.

---

## Anchoring

**What it is**: Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered when making a decision.

**Detection questions**:
- Is the first number/option presented influencing all subsequent comparisons?
- Was the anchor set by someone with an interest in the outcome?

**Counter**: Generate your own estimate before looking at anchors. Deliberately consider options far outside the anchored range.

---

## Availability Heuristic

**What it is**: Overweighting recent, vivid, or easily recalled events when estimating probability.

**Detection questions**:
- Are you overestimating a risk because of a recent failure in your network?
- Are you overestimating an opportunity because of a recent success story you heard?
- Does the frequency of news coverage affect your probability estimate?

**Counter**: Seek out base rates and historical data. Vivid examples are memorable but not representative.

---

## Authority Bias

**What it is**: Over-deferring to someone's opinion because of their title, status, or expertise — even when their expertise doesn't apply.

**Detection questions**:
- Is the main argument "the CEO / investor / expert thinks we should"?
- Does the authority figure have direct experience with *this specific type* of decision?
- Would you make the same call if the authority figure held the opposite view?

**Counter**: Separate the argument from its source. Evaluate the reasoning, not the person making it.

---

## Loss Aversion

**What it is**: Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. This causes people to avoid losses more than they pursue equivalent gains.

**Detection questions**:
- Are you avoiding a reasonable risk because the downside would *feel* bad, even if the expected value is positive?
- Are you framing the decision as "I might lose X" rather than "I might gain Y"?

**Counter**: Reframe losses as foregone gains. Calculate expected value rather than feeling your way through the downside.

---

## Halo Effect

**What it is**: Letting one positive (or negative) attribute about a person, company, or idea color your assessment of everything else about them.

**Detection questions**:
- Are you approving this plan because you like the person presenting it?
- Are you dismissing this vendor because you had a bad experience with them on an unrelated issue?
- Would you evaluate this proposal differently if a different person had written it?

**Counter**: Evaluate options independently of their source. Separate person assessment from idea assessment.

---

## Groupthink

**What it is**: Group conformity suppresses dissent, leading to a false consensus that no individual member actually holds.

**Detection questions**:
- Did anyone disagree? If not, why not?
- Were dissenting views heard, or were they dismissed before being considered?
- Did the group converge too quickly?

**Counter**: Assign a formal devil's advocate role. Conduct pre-mortems. Collect written opinions before group discussion.
