# Decision Frameworks Reference

Used by `/decision-toolkit`. Apply these frameworks to structured decision analysis.

---

## 1. First Principles

**What it is**: Break a decision down to its fundamental truths, ignoring assumptions and conventions.

**When to use**: When the default answer is "because we've always done it this way" or when industry conventions feel like constraints.

**How to apply**:
1. State the decision as a question.
2. List all the assumptions currently baked into the framing.
3. For each assumption, ask: "Is this actually true, or is it an inherited convention?"
4. Rebuild the decision from the facts that remain.

**Example**: "We need a sales team to sell enterprise." First principles: what does selling enterprise actually require? (1) Someone to answer questions (2) Trust signals (3) Contract negotiation. Do all three require a full-time sales hire, or could some be handled differently?

---

## 2. Second-Order Thinking

**What it is**: Go beyond the immediate consequence of a decision to what happens *next*.

**When to use**: When a decision seems obviously good — the obvious choice often hides a second-order cost.

**How to apply**:
- First-order: "If I do X, what immediately happens?"
- Second-order: "And then what? What new problems does success create?"
- Third-order (rarely needed): "And after that?"

**Example**: Hiring quickly to close a gap. Second-order: a bad hire is harder to undo than a gap — morale costs, performance management costs, rehiring costs. Third-order: a bad hire who stays sets a quality bar that attracts more bad hires.

---

## 3. Regret Minimization (Bezos Framework)

**What it is**: Project yourself to age 80 and ask which choice you'd regret more.

**When to use**: When two options are roughly equal on the numbers but feel different emotionally. When fear of failure is masking the right call.

**How to apply**:
1. Imagine yourself at 80 looking back on this moment.
2. Which choice — if you don't make it — will you regret having missed?
3. Which choice — if it fails — will you regret having made?
4. The answer that minimizes total regret across both questions is usually correct.

**Key insight**: We regret inaction more than action over long time horizons.

---

## 4. Two-Way Door vs. One-Way Door

**What it is**: Distinguish reversible decisions (two-way door) from irreversible ones (one-way door).

**When to use**: Before deciding how much time and analysis to invest in a decision.

**Framework**:
- **Two-way door**: Can be undone within weeks/months with acceptable cost. Optimize for speed, learning, and action. Overthinking two-way doors is waste.
- **One-way door**: Cannot be undone, or reversal is prohibitively expensive. Slow down, gather more data, consult more people.

**Examples**:
- Launching a new feature: usually two-way (can roll back)
- Firing a key employee: one-way (can't undo the relationship damage)
- Signing a 3-year enterprise contract: one-way (or very expensive to exit)
- Changing your pricing page: two-way (can change it back)

## 5. Pre-mortem

**What it is**: Imagine the decision has already been made AND has already failed. Work backward to find why.

**When to use**: Before committing to a major one-way door. When everyone in the room seems too optimistic.

**How to apply**:
1. State: "Assume it's 12 months from now and [Option A] failed completely. What happened?"
2. Have each stakeholder write down the most plausible failure story independently.
3. Share and discuss. The recurring themes are your highest-priority risks.
4. Ask: given these risks, does the decision change? Or do we just need mitigation?

**Why it works**: It gives people permission to voice concerns they'd suppress in a normal planning meeting.

---

## 6. WRAP Framework (Heath Brothers)

**What it is**: A four-step process for overcoming the biases that corrupt decision-making.

**Steps**:
- **W — Widen your options**: Are there more than 2 options? The binary "do X or don't" is almost always a false choice. "What else could we do?"
- **R — Reality-test your assumptions**: Run a small experiment. Find someone who's done what you're considering. Look for disconfirming evidence.
- **A — Attain distance before deciding**: Separate yourself from short-term emotion. "What would I tell my best friend to do in this situation?"
- **P — Prepare to be wrong**: Set a tripwire — a predetermined condition that triggers reassessment. "If [metric] hasn't improved by [date], we revisit."

## 7. 10-10-10 (Suzy Welch)

**What it is**: Evaluate a decision across three time horizons.

**How to apply**:
- How will I feel about this decision in **10 minutes**?
- How will I feel about this decision in **10 months**?
- How will I feel about this decision in **10 years**?

**When to use**: When short-term discomfort (a hard conversation, a price increase, a firing) is clouding judgment about long-term benefit.

**Key insight**: Decisions that feel terrible in 10 minutes often look correct in 10 months and irrelevant in 10 years.

---

## 8. Expected Value with Asymmetric Upside

**What it is**: Assign probabilities to outcomes and calculate expected value — but also check for asymmetry.

**How to apply**:
1. List all plausible outcomes for each option.
2. Assign rough probabilities (even very rough is better than nothing).
3. Assign a value to each outcome (financial, strategic, or gut-scaled 1–10).
4. Calculate: Expected Value = Σ(probability × value) for each option.
5. Check for **asymmetric upside**: an option with lower expected value but a possible 10x outcome may warrant taking the risk.

**Key insight**: Expected value analysis is most useful for reversible decisions made repeatedly. For irreversible or rare decisions, also weight the downside scenario heavily — avoid ruin at the cost of upside.
