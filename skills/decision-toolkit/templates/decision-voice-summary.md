# Voice Summary Template

5–7 paragraphs designed for audio consumption — no bullet points, no headers in the final output. Pure prose, meant to be read aloud or listened to on the go.

## When to use

Output format **3 (Voice Summary)** from `decision-toolkit` Step 4. Use when the user is away from their desk, prefers spoken summaries, or wants to share the analysis verbally with someone else.

---

## Structure

**Paragraph 1 — The Decision**
State the decision being faced in one clear sentence. Include who is making it and by when.

> "You're deciding [decision statement]. The deadline is [deadline], and the default — if no decision is made — is [default path]."

**Paragraph 2 — Context and Pressure**
What triggered this decision? What constraints exist? What does success look like in 6 months?

> "This came to a head because [trigger]. The key constraint is [constraint]. If it goes well, [success in 6 months]."

**Paragraph 3 — Core Tensions**
What are the 2–3 central trade-offs? What do you gain and give up with each main path?

> "The tension here is [trade-off A vs. B]. Choosing [option A] means gaining [X] but giving up [Y]. Choosing [option B] means [Z], at the cost of [W]."

**Paragraph 4 — What the Frameworks Revealed**
Which lenses surfaced something surprising? What did the bias audit flag? What did the pre-mortem uncover?

> "The analysis surfaced a few things worth noting. [First insight — e.g., the sunk cost bias is showing up here]. The pre-mortem scenario — where this fails — looks like [brief description], and it's more likely than it first appears."

**Paragraph 5 — The Open Questions**
The 3 questions the decision-maker must sit with. Not answered — held.

> "Before making the call, three questions are worth sitting with. [Question 1]. [Question 2]. [Question 3]. None of these have easy answers — that's why they matter."

**Paragraph 6 — Directional Read** *(only include if the user asked for a recommendation)*
One clear direction, one key risk, one condition that would change the read.

> "Based on the analysis, [direction] looks strongest — primarily because [one reason]. The main risk to watch is [risk]. That said, this recommendation changes if [condition]."

**Paragraph 7 — Next Step**
One concrete action before the deadline. Not the decision itself — the step that makes the decision clearer or easier.

> "The one move to make before [deadline] is [specific action]. That will either confirm the direction or surface the information that's still missing."

---

## Word Count Targets

| Section | Target |
|---------|--------|
| Full summary | 400–600 words |
| Each paragraph | 60–100 words |
| Core tensions paragraph | 80–120 words |

---

## Writing Rules

- No bullet points — prose only
- No headers in the final output — it must flow as spoken text
- Use "you" not "one" — speak directly to the decision-maker
- Spell out numbers under 10, use numerals for 10 and above
- Avoid framework jargon — translate "pre-mortem" into "what a failure would look like"
- Use the specific names, numbers, and details from the conversation — specificity makes it real
- If the decision has a deadline, mention it in paragraphs 1 and 7

---

## Example opening

*Decision: Should I raise my consulting prices by 30% starting next quarter?*

> "You're deciding whether to raise your consulting prices — specifically by 30%, starting next quarter. The deadline is the end of this month, when your next client contract comes up for renewal. If you don't make a call, the default is to keep current pricing, which means another year at rates that no longer reflect your market position or the value you're delivering."
